Medetomidine in the Illicit Drug Supply

Lynch MJ, Pizon AF, Yealy DM. Ann Emerg Med. 2026;87:709-716.

Implications for emergency care and withdrawal management

Clinical concepts reviewEarly / limited evidenceToxicology + addiction medicineASAM 3.7 / 3.5 relevance

Clinical question

How should clinicians recognize and manage medetomidine-associated intoxication and withdrawal when it appears with illicit opioids?

What the paper says

  • Medetomidine is a veterinary alpha-2 agonist increasingly detected with illicit fentanyl.
  • Naloxone may restore ventilation while medetomidine sedation persists.
  • Withdrawal can begin within 4-6 hours, progress rapidly, and include severe autonomic instability, vomiting, agitation, delirium, and hyperthermia.
  • Management described by the authors uses early antiemetic therapy, oral/transdermal alpha-2 agonists, and IV dexmedetomidine for refractory cases.

Evidence guardrails

  • This is a narrative review/clinical-concepts paper, not a randomized treatment trial or validated guideline.
  • Medetomidine-specific evidence comes largely from case reports, case series, pharmacology, and expert experience.
  • Illicit exposures are polysubstance exposures; attribution and testing are limited.
  • Protocol doses require local toxicology, pharmacy, emergency, and ICU review.
Do not miss: An unusually early, severe withdrawal picture should not automatically be treated as opioid withdrawal based only on COWS. Consider medetomidine or another adulterant, reassess physiology and timing, and obtain toxicology/poison-center support.

Facilitator discussion

The most useful discriminator is the time course and physiologic pattern: medetomidine withdrawal can begin unusually soon, with prominent vomiting, tremor, hypertension, tachycardia, agitation, delirium, and hyperthermia. Opioid withdrawal may overlap, while benzodiazepine withdrawal more often centers on anxiety, perceptual change, seizures, and delirium; stimulant toxicity tends toward sympathomimetic activation; xylazine can produce sedation, bradycardia, hypotension, and its own withdrawal syndrome. None is reliably diagnosed by a single score, and polysubstance exposure means the team should document a working differential rather than force one label.

COWS is insufficient when symptoms are early, atypical, refractory to expected opioid treatment, or dominated by autonomic instability. The feasible core is structured monitoring, ECG/electrolytes, antiemetic access, alpha-2 agonist protocols, clear ICU/toxicology escalation, and separate reassessment for MOUD timing. When testing is unavailable, document suspected exposure as a clinical syndrome with last known use, response to naloxone and opioid-directed therapy, vital-sign trajectory, exam findings, co-exposures, consultant input, and uncertainty. Discharge planning should include naloxone, not using alone, safer-supply and sterile-equipment counseling, return precautions, and linkage to MOUD. The highest-value prospective evidence would combine a validated withdrawal scale with a treatment registry, confirmatory toxicology, and comparative protocols.

Clinical implications for ASAM 3.7/3.5 practice

Operational takeaway: Ask about changing drug-supply effects when a patient has prolonged sedation after naloxone or develops unusually early, severe, refractory autonomic withdrawal. Arrange monitoring that matches the trajectory, not just the initial presentation.
  • Build medetomidine awareness into intake and handoff language without assuming the patient can identify the adulterant.
  • Do not use opioid withdrawal as the only explanatory label for early severe symptoms.
  • Maintain an escalation pathway to emergency medicine, medical toxicology, poison center, ICU, pharmacy, and addiction medicine.
  • Separate acute stabilization from eventual MOUD initiation; reassess physiology, timing, opioid exposure, and withdrawal phenotype.
  • Provide return precautions for delayed vomiting, diaphoresis, tremor, agitation, chest pain, or vital-sign abnormalities.
