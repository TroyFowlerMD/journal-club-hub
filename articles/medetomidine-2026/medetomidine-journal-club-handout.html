Clinical question

How should clinicians recognize and manage medetomidine-associated intoxication and withdrawal when it appears with illicit opioids?

What the paper says

  • Medetomidine is a veterinary alpha-2 agonist increasingly detected with illicit fentanyl.
  • Naloxone may restore ventilation while medetomidine sedation persists.
  • Withdrawal can begin within 4-6 hours, progress rapidly, and include severe autonomic instability, vomiting, agitation, delirium, and hyperthermia.
  • Management described by the authors uses early antiemetic therapy, oral/transdermal alpha-2 agonists, and IV dexmedetomidine for refractory cases.

Evidence guardrails

  • This is a narrative review/clinical-concepts paper, not a randomized treatment trial or validated guideline.
  • Medetomidine-specific evidence comes largely from case reports, case series, pharmacology, and expert experience.
  • Illicit exposures are polysubstance exposures; attribution and testing are limited.
  • Protocol doses require local toxicology, pharmacy, emergency, and ICU review.
Do not miss: An unusually early, severe withdrawal picture should not automatically be treated as opioid withdrawal based only on COWS. Consider medetomidine or another adulterant, reassess physiology and timing, and obtain toxicology/poison-center support.

Professional-context update: CDC clinician materials emphasize that atipamezole is not an established human reversal strategy and may precipitate dangerous withdrawal. CDC and UPMC education also reinforce toxicology consultation, recognition of refractory vomiting/autonomic instability, and the evolving nature of treatment evidence.
