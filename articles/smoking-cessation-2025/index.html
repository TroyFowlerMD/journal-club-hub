Overview

Clinical Question

Among US adults with a history of substance use disorder, is within-person transition from current to former cigarette smoking associated with sustained SUD recovery over time, after accounting for between-person confounders?

Study Characteristics

Study Design
Longitudinal Cohort, Fixed-Effects Logistic Regression
Data Source
PATH Study, nationally representative
Sample Size
2,652 adults, 9,088 observations
Follow-Up
4 waves, 2013–2018
Exposure
Cigarette smoking status
Outcome
SUD recovery via GAIN-SS
Evidence Level
Level III — Observational

Background

  • 48.5 million US adults have an SUD in the past 12 months
  • 35.8% of those with an SUD currently smoke — disproportionately high tobacco-related mortality
  • Tobacco use is undertreated in SUD treatment facilities — often considered "low priority"
  • Treatment professionals may defer tobacco cessation, believing it threatens primary recovery
  • Directly relevant to ASAM 3.7/3.5 residential settings where tobacco policies vary widely

Key Results

Primary Cohort (Waves 1–4, 2013–2018)

Model OR (Former vs Current) 95% CI P Value
Unadjusted (Model 1) 1.42 1.18–1.71 <.001
Adjusted (Model 2) 1.30 1.07–1.57 .009

Key finding: Quitting smoking was associated with a 30% increase in odds of SUD recovery after adjusting for time-varying covariates and all between-person differences.

Sensitivity Analyses

Analysis Adjusted OR 95% CI P Value
Lagged Model (smoking change precedes recovery) 1.43 1.00–2.05 .04
Second Cohort (W4–W7, 2016–2023, spans COVID-19) 1.37 1.13–1.66 .002
  • Lagged model: Confirms temporal ordering — smoking cessation preceded recovery improvement (OR 1.43, P = .04)
  • Second cohort replication: Association persisted across an independent cohort spanning the COVID-19 pandemic (OR 1.37, P = .002)

Other Significant Associations with SUD Recovery

Variable OR 95% CI P Value
Time (positive trend) 1.11 1.05–1.17 <.0001
Mental health improvement (PROMIS) 1.04 1.03–1.06 <.0001
Never smokers vs current 1.72 0.36–8.35 .50
  • Time: Recovery rates improved across study waves (OR 1.11 per wave)
  • Mental health: PROMIS improvement independently associated with recovery (OR 1.04 per unit)
  • Never smokers vs current: Not statistically significant — small cell size (8.5% never smokers) limits inference

Methods Deep Dive

Fixed-Effects Logistic Regression

  • Core principle: Each person serves as their own control — the model estimates the association between within-person changes in smoking status and within-person changes in recovery status
  • Eliminates all time-invariant confounders — both measured (race, sex, education at baseline) and unmeasured (genetic predisposition, baseline substance type, prior treatment history, personality traits)
  • Approximates experimental conditions for observational data by leveraging longitudinal within-person variation only
  • Requires within-person change: Individuals who remained stable across all waves (consistently recovered or consistently not) are excluded from the estimating sample — the model only uses information from individuals who changed status

Why This Design Matters

  • Standard logistic regression (between-person) cannot rule out that healthier/more motivated people both quit smoking and recover from SUD
  • Fixed-effects removes this "healthy quitter" confound by comparing each person to themselves over time
  • Remaining concern: unmeasured time-varying confounders (e.g., life events that simultaneously improve all health behaviors)

Time-Varying Covariates Controlled

  • Mental health status (PROMIS Global Mental Health scale)
  • Educational attainment changes
  • Physician visits (past 12 months)
  • Emergency department visits (past 12 months)
  • Smoking cessation medication use (varenicline, bupropion)
  • Urban/rural residence changes

Outcome Measurement

  • GAIN-SS (Global Appraisal of Individual Needs — Short Screener) — validated instrument for SUD symptom assessment
  • Recovery definition: High lifetime symptoms (score 4–7) + zero past-year symptoms = sustained remission
  • Binary classification: in recovery vs. current SUD/returned to use

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  1. Nationally representative sample (PATH Study) — generalizable to millions of US adults with SUD
  2. Fixed-effects regression removes all time-invariant between-person confounders (approximates experimental conditions)
  3. Robust across multiple sensitivity analyses (lagged models, second cohort)
  4. Adjusted for relevant time-varying covariates including mental health changes and smoking cessation medications
  5. Uses validated GAIN-SS measure of SUD recovery
  6. Longitudinal design with annual assessments over 4 waves
  7. Second cohort spanned COVID-19 pandemic — association persisted
  8. Population-based (includes treated and untreated individuals)

Limitations

  1. No direct measure of SUD treatment or anti-craving medications
  2. All measures self-reported
  3. Recovery defined as abstinence only — does not capture harm reduction outcomes
  4. Potential unmeasured time-varying confounders
  5. Cannot establish causation despite accounting for temporal order in sensitivity analyses
  6. Never-smoking category had small cell size (8.5%), limiting inference
  7. Does not assess e-cigarettes or other nicotine products
  8. Fixed-effects design requires within-person change — excludes stable individuals

Clinical Implications for ASAM 3.7/3.5 Practice

This evidence directly challenges the "one thing at a time" philosophy. Quitting smoking is not separate from recovery — it is part of recovery.

Recommended Practice Changes

  • Integrate tobacco treatment into standard SUD care — not as an add-on, not deferred to post-discharge
  • Offer NRT, varenicline, and/or bupropion to all smoking patients in residential treatment as first-line pharmacotherapy
  • Staff education: Smoking cessation does not threaten recovery — it supports it (OR 1.30, P = .009)
  • Smoke-free facility policies supported by this evidence base
  • Patient education: Use motivational interviewing to frame quitting smoking as part of recovery, not separate from it
  • Track dual outcomes: Monitor both SUD recovery and smoking status as treatment outcomes
  • Population-level approaches: Support tobacco quit-line referrals, consistent tobacco taxation, and smoke-free policies

Context

  • 35.8% of adults with SUD currently smoke — tobacco use remains undertreated in most SUD treatment facilities
  • Many residential programs defer tobacco cessation or lack tobacco treatment services entirely
  • Staff may believe addressing smoking will compromise primary SUD recovery — this study provides Level III evidence that the opposite is true
  • Renewed calls to integrate smoking cessation into all SUD treatment (Kleinman & Barnett, JAMA Psychiatry 2024)

Discussion Questions

1

Does our current facility policy support or hinder tobacco cessation during residential treatment? What specific changes would bring us into alignment with this evidence?

2

Given that 35.8% of adults with SUD currently smoke, what is our facility's rate, and are we systematically assessing and treating tobacco use disorder?

3

This study found that mental health improvement independently predicted recovery (OR 1.04). How does this inform our approach to co-occurring psychiatric care during SUD treatment?

4

The "one thing at a time" belief persists despite this evidence. What institutional or cultural barriers prevent us from integrating tobacco treatment, and how do we address them?

5

How should we frame simultaneous tobacco cessation to patients who believe it will compromise their primary recovery? What motivational interviewing approaches work best?

6

The study used abstinence as the recovery measure. Should we also consider harm reduction metrics (e.g., reduction in use) when evaluating our treatment outcomes?
