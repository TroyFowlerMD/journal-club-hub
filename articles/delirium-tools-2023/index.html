Overview

Clinical Question

In hospitalized older adults, which bedside delirium screening tool — the 4AT, Nu-DESC, or CAM — offers the best balance of diagnostic accuracy and clinical feasibility?

Study Design

Systematic review and meta-analysis of diagnostic test accuracy studies. Databases: PubMed, Embase, CINAHL, ProQuest, PsycINFO (inception to Sept 7, 2022). Combined with CAM-ICU worksheet (Ely, 2002; AACN adaptation, 2013).

Tools Compared

4AT (4 'A's Test)

Components
Alertness, AMT-4, Attention (months backward), Acute change/fluctuation
Scoring
0–12; ≥4 = possible delirium; 1–3 = cognitive impairment; 0 = unlikely
Administration Time
~2 minutes
Assessor
Any healthcare provider
Key Advantage
Can assess uncooperative/untestable patients; designed for acute settings
Studies in Meta-Analysis
11 studies, 2,627 patients

Nu-DESC (Nursing Delirium Screening Scale)

Components
5 items — disorientation, inappropriate behavior, inappropriate communication, illusions/hallucinations, psychomotor retardation
Scoring
0–10 (each item 0–2); ≥2 = delirium
Administration Time
2–3 minutes (observation-based)
Assessor
Nurses via observation during daily care
Key Advantage
Observation-based; detects hypoactive delirium (psychomotor retardation item); integrates into routine nursing workflow
Studies in Meta-Analysis
7 studies, 1,590 patients

CAM (Confusion Assessment Method)

Components
4 features — (1) acute onset/fluctuating course, (2) inattention, (3) disorganized thinking, (4) altered LOC
Diagnostic Algorithm
Features 1 AND 2 present + Feature 3 OR 4
Administration Time
Longer administration (variable)
Assessor
Requires specific training; best with physicians/researchers
Key Advantage
Highest specificity (0.99); well-validated including in dementia populations
Studies in Meta-Analysis
20 studies (17 unique), 3,002 patients

CAM-ICU (ICU Adaptation)

Algorithm
Same 4-feature CAM algorithm adapted for non-verbal/intubated patients
Feature 1
Alteration/fluctuation in mental status (baseline comparison + RASS/GCS fluctuation)
Feature 2 — Inattention
Letters Attention Test (SAVEAHAART); >2 errors = positive
Feature 3 — Altered LOC
RASS ≠ 0 or SAS ≠ 4
Feature 4 — Disorganized Thinking
Yes/No questions + command following; >1 combined error = positive
Frequency
Every shift (q8–12h); endorsed by SCCM 2013 PAD guidelines

Diagnostic Accuracy

Pooled Sensitivity (Ability to Detect Delirium)

4AT 0.76 (0.63–0.85)
Nu-DESC 0.78 (0.56–0.91)
CAM 0.80 (0.67–0.89)

No significant difference between tools (all P > 0.85)

Pooled Specificity (Ability to Rule Out Delirium)

4AT 0.90 (0.85–0.93)
Nu-DESC 0.90 (0.87–0.92)
CAM 0.99 (0.97–0.99)

Strengths & Limitations

Strengths

  1. First meta-analysis directly comparing all 3 tools head-to-head
  2. Used suggested cutoff values (not optimized) — reduces overestimation of diagnostic accuracy
  3. Required DSM/ICD as gold standard reference in all included studies
  4. Large combined sample (7,378 patients across 30 studies)
  5. QUADAS-2 quality assessment performed on all included studies
  6. No significant publication bias detected for any tool

Limitations

  1. Significant between-study heterogeneity for all tools
  2. Unequal sample sizes across tools (4AT: 11, Nu-DESC: 7, CAM: 20 studies)
  3. Some studies had unclear assessor blinding
  4. Limited Nu-DESC data in dementia populations
  5. Most studies in older adults (mean age >65); may not generalize to younger SUD populations
  6. No studies specifically in substance withdrawal delirium

Clinical Implications for ASAM 3.7/3.5 Practice

Transfer protocol: Confirmed delirium → immediate physician assessment → transfer to ASAM 4.0 (Mission Hospital, unless on diversion)

Recommended Two-Stage Screening Approach

  • Stage 1 — Nursing screen (Nu-DESC or 4AT): Routine q-shift assessment for all patients in active withdrawal
    • Nu-DESC preferred: Observation-based, integrates into routine nursing care, detects hypoactive delirium
    • 4AT alternative: Better for uncooperative or acutely intoxicated patients
  • Stage 2 — Physician CAM confirmation: Any positive screen triggers formal CAM by physician/trained clinician
    • CAM specificity (0.99) optimal for confirming diagnosis before initiating transfer
    • Physician administration essential — CAM sensitivity drops to 25% with untrained assessors

Special Considerations for SUD Populations

  • Alcohol withdrawal DT timeline: Delirium tremens onset typically 48–96 hours after last drink — heighten screening during this window
  • Benzodiazepine withdrawal: Similar timeline to alcohol; can be prolonged; monitor closely in patients on taper protocols
  • Wernicke encephalopathy DDx: Must remain on differential — classic triad (confusion, ophthalmoplegia, ataxia) overlaps with delirium; thiamine replacement should not await diagnostic confirmation
  • Polypharmacy / polysubstance: Complicates assessment; intoxication states may mimic delirium features (altered LOC, inattention)
  • Age generalizability: Meta-analysis populations mean age 72–78; many SUD patients are younger — cutoff performance may differ

Staff Training Emphasis

  • CAM sensitivity drops from 86% → 25% when administered by nurses without specific training (P = 0.00004)
  • Training programs are essential for any CAM-based protocol
  • Structured delirium screening results should be part of routine clinical documentation for all at-risk patients (alcohol/BZD withdrawal, elderly, medically complex)
  • Account for high staff turnover in residential settings — build training into onboarding

Discussion Questions

1

Given that our nursing staff performs most bedside assessments, should we implement the Nu-DESC as a routine q-shift screening tool for all patients in active withdrawal? What training would be required?

2

The CAM's sensitivity drops dramatically when administered by nurses without specific training (25% vs 86%). How do we ensure adequate staff training on delirium recognition while managing turnover in residential settings?

3

At what point in the withdrawal trajectory should we increase screening frequency? Should CIWA scores above a certain threshold automatically trigger formal delirium screening?

4

When a patient screens positive, what is our current time-to-transfer to Mission Hospital (ASAM 4.0)? Are there system-level barriers to expediting this?

5

Should we consider implementing a two-stage protocol — routine Nu-DESC nursing screens with physician CAM confirmation — rather than relying on a single tool?

6

These tools were validated primarily in geriatric populations. How confident are we in applying these cutoffs to a younger SUD population with acute withdrawal states?
